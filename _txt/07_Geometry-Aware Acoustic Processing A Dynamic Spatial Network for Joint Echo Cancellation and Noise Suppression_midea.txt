ABSTRACT
We propose a geometry-agnostic solution for multichannel acoustic echo cancellation and noise suppression. The
model employs a Dynamic Spatial Encoder that adaptively
processes spatial information from arbitrary microphone arrays, combined with powerful separation modules for echo
cancellation and noise suppression. A post-processing network further enhances output quality by eliminating residual
artifacts and speech distortions. The system incorporates
an integrated classifier for frame-level voice activity detection, enabling accurate identification of acoustic states
(double-talk, far-end single-talk, near-end single-talk and
double-silence). Experimental results demonstrate consistent performance across various array geometries for both
stationary and moving speakers, achieving significant word
error rate (WER) reduction in challenging conditions while
improving perceptual speech quality.
Index Terms— Acoustic echo cancellation, multichannel, geometry-agnostic
1. INTRODUCTION
Acoustic echo cancellation (AEC) addresses the critical challenge of removing undesired echoes caused by loudspeakermicrophone coupling in communication systems [1]. Modern conference rooms, typically equipped with multiple
microphones and loudspeakers, require multichannel solutions (MCAEC) and multi-loudspeaker echo cancellation
(MMAEC) approaches. These scenarios present both additional challenges and opportunities compared to singlechannel AEC, driving significant research attention [2].
While neural network-based multichannel AEC methods [3, 4, 5, 6] have demonstrated superior performance over
conventional approaches[7, 8, 9], their real-world deployment
faces a critical limitation: current models require retraining
for different microphone array geometries. This constraint

necessitates impractical computational resources when adapting to diverse conference room configurations. Recent arrayagnostic speech enhancement methods [10, 11, 12, 13, 14]
have explored this direction. DeFTAN-AA [12] introduces
a spatial transformer that aggregates spatial information
through channel-wise self-attention and a space-object crossattention (SOCA) block to mitigate array-specific overfitting.
Similarly, Zheng et al. [14] proposes a spatial filter bank to
extract geometry-invariant features, processed by a two-stage
conformer-based model. ArrayDPS [13] addresses the blind
speech separation (BSS) problem through an unsupervised,
array-agnostic framework that leverages diffusion posterior
sampling to approximate room acoustics without requiring
prior knowledge of array geometry.
Building upon these advances, we formulate AEC as a
supervised speech separation task. Our framework introduces
three key components: (1) a Dynamic Spatial Encoder that
adaptively processes spatial information from arbitrary microphone arrays, (2) powerful separation modules for joint
echo cancellation and noise suppression, and (3) a postprocessing network that eliminates residual artifacts and
speech distortions. The system incorporates an auxiliary
classification network for frame-level acoustic state detection, identifying four distinct scenarios: double-talk, far-end
single-talk, near-end single-talk and double-silence. This
state information guides the post mask estimation network to
optimize echo cancellation performance. By estimating nearend speech from microphone signals using far-end references
as conditional information, our approach decouples system
performance from specific spatial configurations.
Experiments show that the proposed method effectively
suppresses both acoustic echoes and background noise, even
under challenging conditions with nonlinear distortions for
adverse SER and both static and moving near-end sources.
Furthermore, downstream ASR evaluation shows significant
WER reduction compared to baseline approaches, confirming
the practical utility of our geometry-agnostic design. Code
and audio examples are available at https://github.
com/jingkangqi/VSAEC.

3. PROPOSED METHOD
The proposed AEC system, illustrated in Figure 1, processes
multi-microphone STFT coefficients M ∈ RF ×T ×2Q and
far-end reference signal X ∈ RF ×T ×2 to estimate the clean
signal ŝ, where F and T denote the number of frequency bins
and time frames, respectively.
The model incorporates a Dynamic Spatial Encoder to
capture spatial correlations between arbitrary microphone
pairs, enabling channel-agnostic processing for variable array
geometries. The encoded microphone features are concatenated with the far-end reference signal, and across the convolutional input layer (T-Conv1d) with a kernel size of 1 and

5 on the frequency and time axes, respectively, and with C
channels, obtaining a hidden representation h0 ∈ RF ×T ×C .
h0 is then processed by interleaved Crossband and Narrowband layers to achieve simultaneous speech separation
and echo cancellation. The Crossband and Narrowband layers follow the same structure as described in [15], selected for
their strong ability to separate speech using complex spatial
information. The output of this processing is h1 ∈ RF ×T ×C .
The features h0 and h1 are concatenated and fed to the
Classifier to predict the acoustic scenario labels. The decoder
consists of a linear layer that maps h0 to the preliminary target STFT coefficients S0 . A postnet is then utilized to recover
speech distortions and remove residual noise in the audio signal. Finally, inverse short-time Fourier transform (iSTFT) is
applied to resynthesize the echo-canceled signal ŝ.
3.1. Dynamic Spatial Encoder
The proposed Dynamic Spatial Encoder, illustrated in Figure 1(b), introduces a geometry-agnostic framework for spatial feature extraction in microphone arrays by adaptively processing input features X ∈ RF ×T ×2Q through a hierarchical
spatial attention mechanism. The encoder first reinterprets
the conventional feature dimensions by treating temporal
frames (T ) as batch dimension, frequency bins (F ) as the
embedding dimension, and microphone channels (2Q) as the
sequence dimension, enabling the generation of a 2Q × 2Q
spatial attention matrix that adaptively captures pairwise microphone interactions independent of array configuration.
The attended features are then enhanced through a feedforward network with layer normalization(LN)[16] to introduce
nonlinear feature transformations. These processed features
are then combined with the reshaped original spectral inputs,
where the real and imaginary components are systematically
separated and reorganized into a (Q, 4, T, F )-dimensional
representation through a learnable upsample convolutional
(US-Conv2d) block consisting of a convolution layer, LN
and parametric rectified linear unit (PReLU) activation[17].
Finally, a mean pooling along the microphone-dimension to
generate consolidated features O ∈ RB×C×T ×F that preserve essential spatial information while remaining adaptable
to arbitrary microphone configurations.
3.2. Classifier
The classifier, illustrated in Figure 1(c), provides frame-level
acoustic scenario detection to guide the post-enhancement
process. The network first projects the concatenated highdimensional features [h0 ; h1 ] ∈ RF ×T ×2C into a lowerdimensional label space through a convolutional block, followed by two successive downsampling convolutional blocks
(DS-Conv2D) that progressively reduce the frequency resolution to F/4, balancing computational efficiency with feature
retention. At the bottleneck, a Dual-Path Recurrent (DPR)

module [18] simultaneously models local band-wise temporal
dependencies and global frame-wise frequency correlations
through its dual-path architecture, effectively capturing essential scenario characteristics with minimal parameters. Then
a DS-Conv2D block projects features to dimension D. The
final classification head consists of a max-pooling operation
along the frequency dimension, a fully-connected layer with
weight matrix Wc ∈ RD×Nclass , and a softmax activation that
generates the posterior probability distribution p̂t ∈ [0, 1]Nclass
for each frame, where t denotes the frame index and Nclass
denotes the number of predefined acoustic scenarios.
3.3. PostNet
The postnet serves as the final refinement stage for the enhanced spectrogram, addressing residual artifacts and improving speech quality. As shown in Figure 1(d), the module
processes three inputs: the primary enhanced spectrogram
S0 ∈ CF ×T , scenario labels L ∈ RT ×Nclass from the classifier,
and the reference channel’s microphone signal Mref ∈ CF ×T .
The post-processing begins with FiLM-conditioned [19]
feature transformation, where the reference signal is modulated according to acoustic scenarios through learned scaling
and shifting parameters:
FiLM(Mref |L) = γ(L) ⊙ Mref + β(L)

(4)

where L ∈ RT ×Nclass represents the frame-wise scenario labels from the classifier, and γ, β are learned transformations
according to the current state. The FiLM-processed features
are then concatenated with S0 along the channel dimension
and fed into a U-Net architecture.
The encoder consists of two convolution block to map the
input spectrum to a high-dimensional and three grouped temporal convolution (GT-Conv) blocks employing depth-wise
separable convolutions with dilation rates (1, 2, 5) to capture
multi-scale temporal dependencies. The bottleneck uses two
DPR blocks. The decoder is the mirror version of the encoder,
ultimately generating the real and imaginary components of a
complex ratio mask (CRM) [20]. This mask is applied to the
reference noisy signal through element-wise multiplication to
produce the final enhanced spectrum Ŝ ∈ CF ×T .

4. EXPERIMENTS
4.1. Dataset
For training, we generate the simulated data by taking a similar way as reported in [23]. The far-end and near-end signals
are chosen from train-clean-100 subset from the LibriSpeech
corpus [24], while the background noise is taken from the
DEMAND dataset [25].
To simulate spatialized speech and noise signals, rooms
are generated by randomly sampled width, depth, and height
from the prescribed intervals of [5, 12] m, [5, 12] m, and [2, 5]
m, respectively. The reverberation time (RT60) of the room
is randomly varied within the range [0.3, 1.2] s. A circular 4-microphone array is employed as reference configurations with 10 cm diameters. The RIR is generated using using
gpuRIR [26]. For static scenarios, the near-end speaker is
positioned at least 0.3 m from walls, while two loudspeakers
are systematically distributed in room corners. For moving
scenarios, the near-end speaker’s start location and moving
direction are randomly sampled in the loop trajectories. The
moving speed is sampled in [1, 1.5] m/s. Note that, when
moving speed is high, the simulated direct-path target signals
may have some clicking noise and other audible artifacts. To
mitigate this problem, the speech signals at two adjacent locations are partial-overlapped and applied with a trapezium
window for both the reverberant signal and direct-path signals [15].
For data augmentation, we simulate realistic acoustic
echo cancellation conditions by modeling both nonlinear
distortions and time-varying transmission effects. Following [27], we simulate loudspeaker nonlinearities through hard
clipping and distortion applied to far-end signals. To model
transmission channel variations, we introduce time-varying

delays by applying temporal shifts randomly sampled from
U(−20, 0) ms to each far-end signal, as proposed in [28],
which forces continuous adaptation during processing. Our
double-talk simulation produces 10-second mixed audio segments containing echo signals and near-end speech, each with
randomized durations between 6-10 seconds.
This dataset encompassed signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
ranging from [-10, 10] dB. We mix the noise and the near-end
speech based on SNR. The signal-to-echo ratio (SER), which
is evaluated during double-talk scenarios, randomly ranges
from [-15, 15] dB. We mix the echo signal and the near-end
speech based on SER. The Root-Mean-Square (RMS) level
of each mixed signal is randomly sampled from [-20, -15] dB.
Meanwhile, the signals’ energy of the near-end and the echo
are calculated and compared to the threshold, judging whether
there is voice at the corresponding branch. Four categories are
obtained, i.e., Double Slience(DS), Far-end single talk(FST),
Near-end single talk(NST), Double Talk(DT), acting as classification labels. All audio mixtures are 10 second-long, with
a sampling rate of 16 kHz. In total, we generate 40 hours
of training data for static speaker scenarios and an additional
40 hours for moving speaker scenarios. Scenario distribution
follows 8:1:1 ratios for DT, FST, and NST conditions.
The test set was simulated using the same methodology
as the training set but with different data sources. Clean
speech signals were extracted from the test-clean subset of
LibriSpeech, while noise samples were selected from the
same dataset used for training. To evaluate generalization
capabilities, we conducted experiments with distinct array
geometries: circular, rectangular, and linear configurations.
The array size was systematically varied, with radius randomly sampled between 5 cm and 20 cm.
4.2. Experimental Setup
The STFT configuration employs a 16 ms (256 samples) Hanning window with 8 ms (128 samples) hop length, yielding
129 frequency bins. The model architecture adopts C =
64 hidden channels and L = 8 separation blocks, following
[15]’s configuration (C ′ = 64, C ′′ = 8) with channel 0 as
reference. The classifier utilizes D = 32 hidden dimension,
while the PostNet contains 16 hidden channels, resulting in
the total parameter count is 0.9M.
The model employs a two-phase training strategy: (1) The
base architecture (without PostNet) trains on 4-channel data
for 100 epochs to develop robust, array-agnostic features for
acoustic echo cancellation; (2) With the base model frozen,
the PostNet fine-tunes for 50 epochs to enhance spectral refinement and mitigate AEC performance degradation caused
by array configuration differences, effectively addressing
residual artifacts and improving speech quality. Training
batch size is 4, using the Adam [29] optimizer with an initial
learning rate of 0.001, which decays exponentially by a factor
of 0.99 per epoch.

4.3. Evaluation Metrics
For evaluation, we employ scenario-specific metrics: SI-SNR
improvement (SISNRi) and wideband PESQ [30] for doubletalk (DT) and near-end single-talk (NST) conditions, echo
return loss enhancement (ERLE) [31] for far-end single-talk
(ST-FE). Additionally, we incorporate word error rate (WER)
as a downstream automatic speech recognition (ASR) performance metric.
5. RESULTS AND ANALYSIS
5.1. Comparative Evaluation
In Table 1, we present a comprehensive comparison between our proposed model and two baseline methods. The
NKF+MVDR, represents a modular approach that combines
a neural Kalman filter (NKF) [32] for per-channel echo cancellation with subsequent MVDR beamforming for spatial
processing [2]. ICRN [5], implements an end-to-end complex spectral mapping solution that jointly models temporal
and spatial dependencies through inplace convolutional recurrent networks for multichannel AEC. For fair comparison,
we have scaled the ICRN model size to match our proposed
architecture’s parameter count while maintaining its original
structural design.

All compared systems are trained on the 4-mic static
dataset and evaluated on randomized 4-mic geometries that
differ from the training configurations, ensuring the array
geometry remains unknown a priori. The test set features
varying SNRs uniformly sampled from 5-10 dB, incorporating challenging acoustic conditions such as reverberation,
additive noise, and nonlinear distortions. Under these conditions, while the NKF+MVDR approach exhibits limited
robustness and the neural-based ICRN framework serves as
a strong baseline, our proposed method consistently achieves
superior performance across all three acoustic scenarios. As
evidenced in Table 1, our method demonstrates significant
advantages in processing unseen spatial configurations, outperforming both NKF+MVDR (by 10.8-11.7 dB SISNRi improvement) and ICRN (by 3.5-4.2 dB SISNRi improvement)
in cross-geometry evaluation scenarios.
The ablation study systematically quantifies the contribution of each component. By eliminating the classifier and directly feeding the reference microphone signal to the PostNet,
we demonstrate that the classifier module plays a crucial role
in echo suppression, with its removal leading to a substantial
5.25 dB decrease in ERLE specifically under far-end singletalk conditions. The PostNet, on the other hand, is shown
to be principally responsible for perceptual quality enhancement, as indicated by systematic reductions in PESQ scores
when deactivated, while concurrently providing a stable 1.0-

6. CONCLUSIONS
This work presents a geometry-agnostic neural network for
multichannel acoustic echo cancellation, featuring a Dynamic
Spatial Encoder for adaptive array processing, joint powerful
separation modules for simultaneous echo and noise suppression, and a postnet enhanced by an auxiliary state classifier.
Experimental results demonstrate robust performance across
diverse microphone configurations, with significant improvements in both speech quality and ASR accuracy, particularly
in challenging scenarios involving nonlinear distortions and
moving sound sources. The proposed solution provides an
effective approach for real-world deployment where array geometries and acoustic conditions vary unpredictably, establishing a new benchmark for flexible AEC systems.
